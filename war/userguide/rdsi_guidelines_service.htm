1. Introduction

This guide concerns Metadata for discovery for datasets and dataset series in the context of INSPIRE (Infrastructure for Spatial Information in Europe) [1]. Metadata for discovery is highly structured and contains a fixed number of elements so that the data it describes can be systematically searched and found. These metadata elements are defined in the INSPIRE Metadata Regulation in [2]. The structure for a specific encoding is defined in the corresponding Guidance Document in [3].

The following instructions focus on the creation of metadata for spatial datasets and spatial data series. Spatial Data Services will be described in separate document. The document is a draft version and has been created in collaboration with Eurostat.

2. Guidelines for Metadata Creation

These guidelines are based on the INSPIRE Metadata Regulation [2]. The following tables list all metadata elements, and each element includes the basic technical details, explanation why the element is required, guidelines on how to fill in the element, and an example. The document introduces extra restrictions or best practices to ensure harmonised metadata, and for some metadata fields it goes beyond the definitions of [2] to assist high search precision and easy evaluation of metadata.

Below you find tables that provide an overview of all elements. It lists the name, the definition based on [2] and whether they are original INSPIRE elements (INSPIRE), INSPIRE elements that have been extended with additional aspects (INSPIRE++) or new elements that have been added (New element).

2.1.1 Resource Title

Metadata element name

Resource Title

Why is this element required?

The title is the most informative element of your metadata record. When done correctly, with one glance the person searching for data understands which dataset he or she has in front of him.

In performing a text search within the RDSI portal, the highest priority will be given to results that find the desired search text in the title.

Obligation

Mandatory

Occurrence

One

Domain

Free text.

Guidelines for how to fill in the entry

Please spend some time thinking about the title.

It must be concise and to the point. It should not contain unexplained acronyms. We suggest a maximum length of 250 characters.

Please be as close as possible to the original title of the resource, in the sense of the ‘official naming’.

If this data is part of a larger project, you might indicate the Project at the end of the title, separated with a comma. In case of Project names, abbreviations are allowed, as long as the rest of the title follows the guidelines above and the abbreviation is spelled out immediately in the abstract.

Examples

Remotely sensed imagery, a series of individual orthorectified scenes (multispectral), IMAGE 2000

2.1.2 Resource Abstract

Metadata element name

Resource Abstract

Why is this element required?

The abstract provides a clear and concise statement that enables the reader to understand the content of the dataset.

When performing a text search many search algorithms will give a high priority will be given to results that find the desired search text in the abstract.

Obligation

Mandatory

Occurrence

One

Domain

Free text.

Guidelines for how to fill in the entry

A succinct description (avoid acronyms, if you use them, explain them) of the dataset to include:
• Brief summary: start with the most important details that summarise your dataset.
• Main attributes: briefly describe the main attributes of your dataset.
• Data sources: List the data sources, focus on the responsible parties.
• Legal references: List the legal references of your data.
• Importance of the work: List the impoarnca eof this work, in which other projects is it used?
• Coverage: A linguistic transcriptions of the extent or location in addition to the bounding box.
NOTE: Many discovery clients represent as a first preview only the first 100 characters or the first sentence of the abstract. Therefore, please summarise the most important details you would like to communicate in this first sentence.

Examples

“This data set contains the location of over 4000 Pan European airports and heliports and the most important runways, taken from different sources.

All the airports and heliports reported by Eurocontrol and those covered by Commission Regulation (EC) No 1358/2003 and related acts (Commission Regulation (EC) No 158/2007, Commission Regulation (EC) No 546/2005) (http://eur-lex.europa.eu/LexUriServ/LexUriServ.do?uri=OJ:L:2003:194:0009:0033:EN:PDF) are depicted as points. The large airports are also depicted as areas (polygons) and include all – except one – of the airports covered by Commission Regulation (EC) No 1358/2003 (and related acts). The most important runways are depicted as polylines. There is also an additional layer including all the heliports available in the EuroRegionalMap v.3.0 from Eurogeographics. Airports can be identified via different codes (including TEN, IATA ICAO airport codes and the codes reported in Commission Regulation (EC) No 1358/2003 and amendments).

Dataset coverage is geographic Europe plus territories outside Europe administrated by Member States of the European Union.”

This choice will be the first you make: depending on the resource type you will be asked to fill different elements.

2.1.4. Resource Locator

Metadata element name

Resource Locator

Why is this element required?

This is the 'navigation section' of your metadata record. From here you can point users to the location (URL) where the data can be downloaded, to related view services or to where additional information about the resource may be provided. Setting up the correct resource locators is important for the connection between the data and the services that provide access to them or for providing additional information concerning the resource.

As you have seen above, the possibilities, and uses of this field, are numerous: information, application, download service view service, you name it. This multiple use comes with a problem: The person reading the metadata will only see an unsorted and uninformative list of links. If a person tries to download the data, he or she would eventually have to go through all the links separately, possibly only to find out that none of the links provided actually gives access to the data. To solve this we suggest three optional additional elements that provide more information: A human-readable title and a description, and a machine-readable function code. This allows providing additional information for the user and to automatically sort and label the locators.

Name:please provide a short title. In brackets after the title please provide a (restricted access) if there are any restrictions to the user group this webpage, application or service is available for.
Description:Please provide a brief description. If there is a restricted access to this service please state for whom it is accessible.
Function:Each of the functions is used in a particular context. The context is given below:
A link to a web page with additional information about the resource, e.g. with additional metadata, should have a Function of ‘information’.
If it is possible to download the resource data then links for how to do this must be provided. These could be any of the following:
• A direct download link that returns the actual data has the function: download.
• A link to an application that allows selecting and downloading data from a large database based on additional criteria such as time, location, etc has the function: download.
• A link to an application that allows selecting data from a large database based on additional criteria such as time, location, etc has the function: download.
• A link to a download service i.e. to the GetCapabilities document (in the case of an OGC WFS) that provides all the details of an existing service from which the data can be obtained (the form of the link is not important; it could be a GetCapabilities document request or point to a XML file within a directory available on the web. The only requirement is that the link returns the service capabilities XML file). This has the function download.

Links to order the data could be provided in two different ways:
• Links pointing to a site for an online order process for obtaining the resource. This link has the function order.
• Link pointing to online instructions for requesting the resource from the provider. This link has the function offlineAccess

If it is possible to view the resource data then links for how to do this must be provided:
• A view application link that allows users to e.g. see the different map layers created from the resource data. This link has the function view.
• A link to a view service i.e. to a GetCapabilities document that provides all the details of an existing web map service from which maps created from the resource can be obtained (again the form of this URL is not important but the link must return the service capabilities XML file). This link has the function view.



For a data series, if it is possible to search for the contained datasets that make up the series then links for how to do this must be provided and could be any of the following:
• A search application link that allows users to search for the datasets. This link points to an application and thus has the function search..
• A link to a discovery service i.e. to a GetCapabilities document that provides all the details of a discovery service from which metadata for the datasets can be obtained. This link has the function discovery.

2.1.5. Parent Identifier

Metadata element name

Parent Identifier

Why is this element required?

This element allows to establish relationships between metadata records, and thus also between the resources that the records describe. Example relationships are between a dataset and a dataset series (see example below), but also between a dataset and a complex information system.

For example, if you create a metadata record for a dataset that is part of a series, then the metadata record of the dataset could contain a pointer to the series to state 'this is the series I am part of'. In other words, the dataset (child) has to point to the series (parent). This can be done through the element Parent Identifier, which is not an INSPIRE element, but helps enormously when dealing with datasets and data series. Imagine the following situation: In a catalogue there is a Metadata Record for a series of datasets IMAGE2000 series of individual orthorectified scenes - multispectral. In the same catalogue there is also a metadata record for each of the more than 400 single images. Currently the catalogue providers are hiding the 400 metadata records describing the images. The reason is simple: Imagine you search for Orthoimagery, and you get back more than 400 results! The records on Image 2000 clutter up the results page and you will not be able to find the information you were really looking for, e.g. the Image 2000 Mosaic, which the catalogue also contains a metadata record for.

If in the future the children could point to their parent, i.e. the Metadata records for datasets could point to the corresponding series, a catalogue client would be able to display the results in a hierarchic manner: First it displays only the data series, and only on request does it display also the metadata records for datasets that are contained in this series.

Obligation

Conditional

Occurrence

Multiple

Domain

Free text

Guidelines for how to fill in the entry

Enter the identifier of the series that your dataset is a part of.

Examples

Data series metadata:
• Title - IMAGE2000 series of individual orthorectified scenes - multispectral
• File Identifier - Image2000_Dataseries_MS.xml

Dataset metadata for a subset of the same resource (i.e. only for the SE2 area of the Image 2000 series):
• Title - IMAGE2000 Product 1 (SE2) multispectral

Other comments

Please note that Parent Identifier is an additional element, it is not part of the required INSPIRE Metadata.

We are currently working on technical solutions that support establishing this relationship: the RDSI editor now provides the possibility to establish the relationship between metadata records, by using the additional ISO element ‘Parent Identifier’. This, however, means you will have to create both records, the one of the parent and the child, in the RDSI metadata editor.

2.1.6. Unique Resource Identifier

Metadata element name

Unique Resource Identifier

Why is this element required?

What is a URI?It is very important to have a value that can uniquely identify reference data. This is done through the Unique Resource Identifier (URI), a value that only exists once, like your Codice Fiscale. This value should not change, as long as the reference data it identifies exists. Only a new version of a dataset with its own identity might get a new identifier (for example IMAGE 2000 and IMAGE 2006). The URI is also used to determine relationships between reference data and the services that allow a user to view or to download the reference data: the service will use the reference data URIs to list all the data it provides access to.

Do I need a different URI for my local copy of reference data?No. The URI describes the resource, not a copy of it. The URI can be best compared to an ISBN of a book: Even if there are multiple copies, they all refer to the same resource, and they all have the same ISBN. Only if I modify the copy, and redistribute this modified copy to others, then I might need to register a new reference dataset which gets a new URI, and, consequently, a new Metadata record.

Can the same resource have multiple URIs? Yes. The existence of multiple identifiers is a fact of life. For example, a person can have multiple tax identification numbers in different countries (e.g. a German tax identification number in Germany, and a Codice Fiscale in Italy), each of which can be used to uniquely identify him.

What is the difference between a URI and a URL? While URI stands for Identifier, a URL stands for Location. URLs are known to all of us browsing through the web. In the context of Linked Data there is a general movement towards using URLs as URIs, which is something that we support. Note, just using a URL as an identifier means you can use a real location, but you do not have to. After all here we use it only as an identifier.

How to build a URI?We distinguish locally and globally unique URIs.
• A locally unique URI is a build through an identifier and a namespace. The identifier is unique e.g. within your action, unit or institution, and the namespace in this case ensures that in combination, namespace and identifier are unique.
• A globally unique URI is a single string (a set of characters) that identifies the resource globally without using the help of a namespace. There are two options to generating a globally Unique Resource Identifier:
1. Using a generated Universally Unique Identifier (UUID), provided through an algorithm that ensures uniqueness. There are online tools users can use to generate such a UUID.
2. Using a URL following a predefined pattern and well-defined structure that ensures uniqueness.

2.2.3 Originating Controlled Vocabulary

Metadata element name

Originating Controlled Vocabulary

Why is this element required?

If keyword Values originate from a controlled vocabulary (like GEMET) then details about that vocabulary are required.

Knowing the controlled vocabulary allows users to fully understand the context of the keyword, as the controlled vocabulary often also provides a definition of the term. If, like in case of GEMET, the controlled vocabulary is a multilingual thesaurus, then it is even possible to request a translation of the keyword. Also, as definitions might change, it is important to add which version of the thesaurus the keyword was taken from.

Metadata element name

Why is this element required?

Defining the co-ordinates of a rectangle that determine the area on a map that relates to the resource allows metadata discovery by geographical area.

Obligation

Mandatory

Occurrence

Multiple

Domain

• East: - 180.00 <= value <= 180.00

Guidelines for how to fill in the entry

To help you find the co-ordinates for your data resource we have produced a list of the bounding-boxes for all European countries. See Appendix II: Geographic Bounding Boxes for details of this list and an explanation on where it came from.

2.4 Temporal Reference of the Resource

This allows users to search for data by date.

Individually all temporal reference elements are conditional but one or more temporal reference must be provided. However, it is important that all metadata records provide this information as complete as possible to aid the performance of a search by date. At least the creation date or the temporal extent must be provided.

To avoid the confusion of different styles and interpretations of dates, these guidelines fix the date (and time) representation, following ISO 8601 Data elements and interchange formats - Information interchange - Representation.

Thus, we recommend to use the date format YYYY-MM-DD where YYYY is the year in the Gregorian calendar, MM is the month of the year between 01 (January) and 12 (December), and DD is the day of the month between 01 and 31. For the expressions with high precision the date format can extended with the time format hh:mm:ss where hh is the number of complete hours that have passed since midnight, mm is the number of complete minutes since the start of the hour, and ss is the number of complete seconds since the start of the minute. The capital letter T is used to separate the date and time components, which then combines into YYYY-MM-DDT hh:mm:ss.

NOTE In case your Metadata editor tool has limitations to express the dates according to these guidelines, they might be temporarily overwritten with different rules. In this case we suggest: If the day and/or month is unknown the 01-01 of that year should be encoded. If no information on the temporal reference is available then insert the 9999-01-01 to indicate that it is unknown. However, please note that this results in false precisions which have to be removed as soon as the tools allow for this

2.4.1 Temporal Extent

Metadata element name

Temporal Extent

Why is this element required?

This is the time period covered by the content of the data resource. For certain data such as Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) data it is most important to understand for which time period this information is available. Other examples are sensor data for meteorological parameters and model results that provide time series with a certain temporal extent.

Obligation

Conditional

Occurrence

One

Domain

The Temporal Extent consists of a Start Date and an End Date.
Start Date and End Date are valid dates.

Guidelines for how to fill in the entry

Please do not express false accuracy in the sense of more precision than the data contains. Depending on the precision you want to express, you can leave out elements of the representation, such as:
• YYYY-MM. To express only the month
• YYYY. To express only the year
Additionally, we also allow for an indeterminate end date: If, for example, a data series is updated every day with the latest information then the day after you have created your metadata record it will be immediately out of date. For this reason a time period which has an end position of indeterminate value 'now' is valid.

Examples

Start Date - 2010-01-15
End Date - 2010-12-15

Start Date - 2010-01-15
End Date - now

Other permissible date formats:
• 2005-01-15T09:10:00
• 2009
• 2005-06

2.5 Quality and Validity of the Resource

Elements that will help the user understand the quality of the available data resource.

2.5.1 Lineage

Metadata element name

Lineage

Why is this element required?

This element contains information on how the data resource was created, the source data used, the main steps and transformations that took place in constructing the actual product from the source data. This is very useful for a user in determining its fitness for purpose.

Obligation

Mandatory

Occurrence

One

Domain

Free text

Guidelines for how to fill in the entry

Include, where applicable, statements on the following (in the given order):
• Source material and providers
• Process history, transformations undergone by the data from source to the actual product
• Method of updating, how to identify the current version and any retired versions
• Data validation and quality assurance: Known issues with the dataset should be mentioned.
The Lineage should be a clear and concise statement that avoid acronyms (if you use them, explain them).

2.5.2 Spatial Resolution

Metadata element name

Spatial Resolution

Why is this element required?

Spatial resolution refers to the level of detail of the spatial data resource. Imagine that a user is interested in finding orthoimagery data with a resolution distance of 25 meters or more. To make this possible this element must be filled in and the information given must be harmonised (otherwise people will enter a unit of measure as many different things i.e. m, meter, metre, km, kilometre, kilometres, etc.).

Obligation

Conditional (Mandatory for datasets and dataset series where a resolution distance can be specified)

Occurrence

Multiple

Domain

The Spatial Resolution consists of an Equivalent Scale or Distance.

Equivalent Scale is an integer.

Distance is a number with an accompanying unit of measure.

Guidelines for how to fill in the entry

Distance is used typically for gridded data and imagery-derived products and represents the resolution distances. Equivalent Scale is usually used for maps or map-derived products.

An equivalent scale is generally expressed as an integer value expressing the scale denominator. For example a map with a scale of 1:25.000 gets the value"25000".

To harmonise the unit of measure and to avoid different spellings, please use m for metre, km for kilometre and dd for decimal degrees.

Examples

Distance:
• 25 m

Equivalent Scale:
• 25000

2.6 Conformity of the Resource

Elements that will help the user understand how the data corresponds to any given implementing rules or specifications.

2.6.1 Specification

Metadata element name

Specification

Why is this element required?

This element contains information on the known legislation (or specifications) against which the resource is evaluated including a date of reference to that legislation.

This is important if you provide your data in compliance with a specific data model or in the context of a particular legislation. For example, if you provide your data according to the INSPIRE data specifications, which means they follow a particular well-known data model.

Obligation

Mandatory

Occurrence

Multiple

Domain

The Specification consists of a Title, Address, Reference Date Type and Reference Date.

Title is free text.

Address is a URL (optional or recommended).

Reference Date Type is taken from a code list provided by ISO 19115 CI_DateTypeCode:
• creation
• publication
• revision

Reference Date is a valid date.

Guidelines for how to fill in the entry

Compliance with INSPIRE
All INSPIRE compliant metadata shall include information on the degree of conformity with the INSPIRE Regulation on Spatial Data Sets and Services (http://eur-lex.europa.eu/LexUriServ/LexUriServ.do?uri=OJ:L:2010:323:0011:0102:EN:PDF).However, if you have not tested the conformance with this specification, please do not list it (see 2.6.2 degree, below).

If you are compliant with this regulation, it is recommended to also list the Guidance documents of the corresponding INSPIRE Data Specifications (e.g. Orthoimagery, Administrative Units, Hydrography). In this case, please use the full title of the guidelines document, as found on the INSPIRE website at http://inspire.jrc.ec.europa.eu/index.cfm/pageid/2, using the pattern: "INSPIRE Data Specification on <Theme Name> - Guidelines".

NOTE The INSPIRE Regulation on Spatial Data Sets and Services and the corresponding guidelines currently only apply to datasets that fall under the Annex I INSPIRE Spatial Data Themes . For datasets that fall under Annex II and III the documents are still in the draft stage, therefore you cannot evaluate conformance of your data against these. Strictly speaking, even in this case you should list the regulation and state that you did not evaluate the conformance (yet). However, because of limitations in the ISO 19115 schema, the solution has been adopted is to not list the INSPIRE Regulation on Spatial Data Sets and Services if it has not been evaluated.

What about amendments?
In case that the specification that you want to state compliance with has been amended, and you want to state compliance with this amendment instead, you have to list the amendment.

What about other specifications?
The specification element is not restricted to INSPIRE, there could be other standards relating particularly to your type of data resource. For example forest fire data may well be evaluated against the Commission Regulation (EC) No 804/94 as regards forest-fire information systems. However, compliance with the Metadata Regulation itself should not be reported here, as these elements are only for compliance of the data

How to fill the title?
For legislation that can be found in Eur-lex (http://eur-lex.europa.eu), the title should be the same as found there. Otherwise the title should be the same as given in the document.

How to fill the date?
Reference Date should be a full date (YYYY-MM-DD).

• Address - http://eur-lex.europa.eu/LexUriServ/LexUriServ.do?uri=OJ:L:2010:323:0011:0102:EN:PDF

EU Water Framework Directive:

Other comments

Please note that Address is an additional element, it is not part of the required INSPIRE Metadata.

NOTE Using free text fields that are meant to be informative bears difficulties for an automatic search: If for example you want to find all datasets that are conformant with a particular specification, then you will only be able to do so if really everybody used the same wording. This is unlikely and such a search would thus return unsatisfactory results. Therefore, in the future we would like to use unique identifiers for the INSPIRE related specifications, that can be easily interpreted by machines. We are currently looking into how such identifiers could be structured.

2.6.2 Degree

Metadata element name

Degree

Why is this element required?

The degree of conformity to the legislation or data specification indicated in Specification (above). You might follow a specification, but not be compliant.

Obligation

Mandatory

Occurrence

One for each Specification that exists

Domain

Boolean:
• true if conformant
• false if not conformant
• null if not evaluated

Guidelines for how to fill in the entry

The data resource should be evaluated against the specification and the result recorded. There are three degrees of conformity with a specification: conformant, not conformant and not evaluated. To express these three degrees you can adopt the following rules:


• When the conformity has been evaluated, it shall be reported as follows: if the evaluation has passed, the degree is conformant (True), otherwise it is not conformant (false).
• The absence of metadata related to the conformity to a particular specification does imply that the conformity has not been evaluated (null).

In other words: if you have not tested the compliance with a particular specification, please do not list the specification.

Examples

true

2.7.1 Conditions Applying to Access and Use

Metadata element name

Conditions Applying to Access and Use

Why is this element required?

The purpose of this element is to describe any conditions applying to access or usage of the resource data. Users that read this metadata need to understand under what conditions they are allowed to access and use the resource.

Obligation

Mandatory

Occurrence

Multiple

Domain

Free text

Guidelines for how to fill in the entry

The description should cover:
• access conditions
• use conditions
• access to the data: steps to be followed to get the data
• how to cite the data

Only general information should be provided here, for detailed information a link to a website or document containing the necessary information should be provided.

A limited number of conditions for access and use inside and outside the Commission should be established: Under what conditions can the data be made available to the European Commission for internal use and basic publishing (text, summary tables, view service, pdf etc.)? Under what conditions the data can be made available to the general public i.e. acknowledgement notices or fees that would need to be paid?

A first set of possible categories have been listed in Appendix VI: Categories for data sharing. This list will have to be discussed and completed based on the feedback received.

If no conditions apply enter 'no conditions apply'.

If conditions are unknown enter 'conditions unknown'.

2.7.2 Limitations on Public Access

Metadata element name

Limitations on Public Access

Why is this element required?

The purpose of this element is to highlight the restrictions of the resource data to the general public. Users that read this metadata may want to know if access to the resource is available to the general public and if not, reasons why access could be denied.

Note that this element describes the reasons for the limitations on public access. The limitations themselves, if any, should be listed under Conditions Applying to Access and Use.

Obligation

Mandatory

Occurrence

Multiple

Domain

Code list provided by the INSPIRE Directive which describes the way in which public access may be limited:
• no limitation
• (a) the confidentiality of the proceedings of public authorities, where such confidentiality is provided for by law
• (b) international relations, public security or national defence
• (c) the course of justice, the ability of any person to receive a fair trial or the ability of a public authority to conduct an enquiry of a criminal or disciplinary nature
• (d) the confidentiality of commercial or industrial information, where such confidentiality is provided for by national or Community law to protect a legitimate economic interest, including the public interest in maintaining statistical confidentiality and tax secrecy
• (e) intellectual property rights
• (f) the confidentiality of personal data and/or files relating to a natural person where that person has not consented to the disclosure of the information to the public, where such confidentiality is provided for by national or Community law
• (g) the interests or protection of any person who supplied the information requested on a voluntary basis without being under, or capable of being put under, a legal obligation to do so, unless that person has consented to the release of the information concerned
• (h) the protection of the environment to which such information relates, such as the location of rare species

Guidelines for how to fill in the entry

Enter the limitation on public access that fits the data.

If there is no limitation on public access, then you have to choose the entry 'no limitation'.

Examples

Your data is not accessible to the general public because your data has underlying licensing restrictions, enter (e) intellectual property rights.

2.8.1 Responsible Party

Metadata element name

Responsible Party

Why is this element required?

If users have any questions relating to the resource found they need information on who to contact and how they can be contacted.

Searching via this field allows users to immediately look for or filter data available from JRC, from EEA or from EUROSTAT. Thus, being restrictive on the names to be used allows us to be much more effective when performing a search.

Obligation

Mandatory

Occurrence

Multiple

Domain

The Responsible Party consists of an Organisation Name and an Email Address.
Organisation Name is free text.
Email Address must be of valid email format.

Guidelines for how to fill in the entry

Fill in each of the organisations responsible for the resource.

The name of the organisation should be given in full, without abbreviations. These names should come from a common codelist to be agreed for all DGs.

For the email we suggest avoiding personal emails as people come and go. Instead use regularly checked functional mail boxes of the unit or project responsible for the resource. This will be for your convenience, as you do not have to update your metadata if a person leaves.

Examples

Organisation Name - European Commission, Joint Research Centre
E-Mail Address - rdsi-team@jrc.ec.europa.eu
